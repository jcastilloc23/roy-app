## Strategic Thesis

Roy wins by owning the most painful operational problem in independent music: **royalty reconciliation.**

The long-term play is to become the **financial operating system for independent music rights** — not just a reporting tool, but the unified ledger that indie labels and publishers rely on to understand, protect, and grow their royalty income.

**Moat over time:**
- Unified royalty ledger across all rights types and platforms
- Data network effects (aggregate anonymous benchmarks → systematic underpayment detection)
- Anomaly detection trained across hundreds of indie label datasets
- First-mover in an underserved segment that incumbents are too old or too enterprise-focused to serve

---

## 1. The Problem Space

The US music industry generates **~$33.5B annually** (2025), yet its royalty infrastructure is deeply broken — especially for independent labels and publishers.

**Core systemic failures:**
- Royalties fragmented across 15+ DSPs, each delivering incompatible CSV/PDF formats
- Payment delays averaging 6–9 months (up to 18 months for some royalty types)
- 20–50% royalty misallocation rate — money owed that never arrives or arrives wrong
- No unified ledger — rights holders have no single view of what they're owed
- Manual reconciliation is the industry standard — spreadsheets, PDF statements, human guesswork
- PROs (ASCAP, BMI, SoundExchange) pay quarterly by paper; no API access for most indie labels

**Hard numbers:**
- **$424M** in unmatched US mechanical royalties held by the MLC (public filing, 2025)
- **$2.5B** estimated globally in "black box" unclaimed royalties
- **$700–800M** in songwriter royalties held up for 5+ years due to CRB appeals (Variety, 2023)

**The result:** Indie labels lose real money every quarter — not because the royalties don't exist, but because the infrastructure to find, track, and recover them doesn't.

---

## 2. Why Now

Three macro triggers make this the right moment to build:

**1. Music Modernization Act / MLC Transition (2021–present)**
The MLC launched in 2021 to administer mechanical royalties digitally. Labels and publishers are still migrating from legacy systems — creating immediate data reconciliation demand and a window before new incumbents solidify.

**2. Catalog Investment Boom**
PE funds and institutional investors are buying music catalogs at scale: Blackstone/Hipgnosis ($2.2B, Jul 2024), KKR, Primary Wave. These buyers need royalty due diligence infrastructure that doesn't exist at accessible price points. This creates a new B2B buyer segment Roy can serve as an upsell.

**3. AI + Streaming Fraud Escalation**
Sony removed 75,000+ AI deepfakes in 2024. The first federal streaming fraud indictment was filed in September 2024 ($10M case, North Carolina). Both trends are accelerating urgency for rights visibility and anomaly detection — which is exactly what Roy provides.

## 4. Target Customer (ICP)

### Ideal Customer Profile

**The company:**
- US independent label — 10–100 artists on roster
- $500K–$5M in annual royalty receipts
- 3–8 person royalty / finance team
- Currently using Excel + PDF workflows, or legacy software (Counterpoint, RoyaltyCloud) older than 5 years
- Located in Los Angeles, Nashville, New York, or Atlanta
- Founder/operator 30–45 years old; tech-forward growth mindset

**The person who buys:**
- Head of Royalties / VP Business Affairs — owns the problem, has or reports to budget authority
- Day-to-day: ingesting DSP statements, managing PRO collections, generating artist statements, tracking advance recoupment

**The person who uses it daily:**
- Royalty Analyst — ingesting CSVs from 10+ DSPs, running manual Excel reconciliations, fielding artist disputes

### Anti-ICP — Don't Target First

| Segment | Why |
|---------|-----|
| Big Three (Universal, Sony, Warner) | Proprietary enterprise systems; 10-year contracts; 2+ year sales cycles |
| PROs (ASCAP, BMI, SESAC) | Partners and infrastructure, not customers |
| Micro DIY artists (<100K streams/yr) | No budget; free tools suffice; high churn |
| Passive catalog PE investors | Infrequent buying; want spot analysis, not subscription |
| International-only labels | US-specific compliance (MLC, CRB) not relevant to them |

### Buying Committee

| Role | Title | Triggered By | What They Need to Say Yes |
|------|-------|--------------|--------------------------|
| **Economic Buyer** | Label Owner / MD / CFO | Audit exposure, artist complaints, money left on table | Recover 5–20% more royalties in 12 months |

## 5. Core Problems — Ranked

### P1: Royalty Transparency ★ PRIMARY — Score: 8.05/10

> *"Independent US labels and publishers struggle to accurately identify and collect all royalties owed because metadata is fragmented across 15+ DSPs and PROs with no unified ledger — resulting in $424M in unmatched US mechanicals, 9-month payment delays, and 20–50% misallocation of earnings."*

**Why this pain persists:**
1. **Fragmentation** — 15+ DSPs, each with different CSV schemas; no unified ledger
2. **Standards gap** — DDEX adoption incomplete; CWR delivery is manual for most indie labels
3. **Incentive misalignment** — Majors don't want indie labels surfacing all their data
4. **Data access** — ASCAP, BMI, SoundExchange offer no API to most indie labels
5. **Cost** — Incumbents price out smaller labels; nothing built for the mid-market
6. **Regulation tail** — CRB appeals delay rate changes for 5+ years

**Current workarounds (Roy displaces these):** Excel + manual DSP CSV downloads; Counterpoint (~1990s architecture); Curve Royalty (UK-focused); Royalti.io (early-stage); quarterly paper PRO statements.

---

### P2: Sync Licensing Speed — Score: 7.50/10

> *"Sync clearance requires 2–4 weeks of manual negotiation across master + composition rights holders — resulting in missed campaign deadlines, $50K+ legal costs per project, and creative compromise."*

**Why it persists:** Dual copyright clearance (master + composition from different owners); no standard pricing; entirely email-chain driven; pre-licensed libraries lack premium tracks.

**Market:** $1.6B US sync in 2025; high per-deal value ($5K–$500K for commercial sync). Adjacent expansion for Roy after P1.

---

### P7: Catalog Valuation — Score: 6.80/10

> *"Royalty streams are hard to verify, inconsistently reported, and require weeks of manual audit — resulting in mispriced deals in a $100B+ music IP market."*

**Natural upsell path:** P1 data (royalty reconciliation history) directly enables P7 (catalog valuation). This is the data flywheel: more labels on Roy → richer benchmarking → more accurate anomaly detection and valuation.

**The "aha moment":** Show $X in unaccounted royalties discovered within 15 minutes of onboarding. This is how the economic buyer justifies the purchase — recovered revenue exceeds product cost.

## 12. Structural Hurdles — Why This Market Is Hard

These are the documented, structural barriers any successful product must navigate. Use this to anticipate where Roy will hit friction.

1. **Metadata fragmentation** — 15+ DSPs use incompatible CSV schemas; DDEX adoption is incomplete. Roy must ingest and normalize heterogeneous data as a core infrastructure capability, not an afterthought.

2. **Opaque PRO infrastructure** — ASCAP, BMI, SoundExchange offer no API access for most indie labels. Payments are quarterly, by paper. Building on top of this requires scraping, manual ingestion, or negotiated enterprise partnerships.

3. **Regulatory complexity (CRB / MLC / DDEX)** — The Copyright Royalty Board sets streaming rates disputed in appeals lasting years ($700–800M held up for 5 years). The MLC is still transitioning. Roy must stay current with regulatory changes that shift what data is available and when.

4. **Incentive misalignment** — Majors and large DSPs have structural incentives not to surface underpayments to indie labels. The "black box" benefits incumbents with the data advantage. Roy is explicitly building against this.

5. **Incumbent inertia** — Most mid-market labels use Excel or tools built pre-2015. Migration is painful: historical data, staff retraining, accounting stack integration. Sales cycles lengthen because switching costs feel high even when ROI is clear. The concierge audit ("show your money before you commit") is the answer.

6. **Information asymmetry** — No public standardized royalty stream data. Catalog valuation requires manual audits ($20K–$100K/engagement). Majors and PE funds have data advantages that indie sellers and artists don't. Roy closing this gap is both the product and the moat.

7. **AI disruption (double-edged)** — AI-generated music threatens revenue displacement and copyright enforcement clarity. Roy needs a clear position: is AI content in scope for rights tracking, or excluded? This decision will become more pressing as AI music scales.

8. **GTM fragmentation** — No single channel reaches all indie labels. Key intermediaries (music attorneys, CPAs, distributors) are the most efficient referral channels but require trust-building and relationship development. Distributor partnerships are the scale path but require product credibility first.

9. **Royalty expiry risk** — PROs expire unclaimed royalties in 2–3 years. The 6–12 month detection lag in current tools means money is permanently lost before labels know it's missing. Roy's real-time detection isn't just a feature — it's the product's core value proposition.

10. **Streaming fraud escalation** — First federal indictment September 2024 ($10M, North Carolina). Platforms are slow to resolve disputes. Anomaly detection and automated dispute generation become table-stakes features as the fraud problem grows.
